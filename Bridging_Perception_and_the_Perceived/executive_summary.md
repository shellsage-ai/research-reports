This report asks whether the gap between perception and the perceived world can be fully closed, and why that matters for knowledge, scientific inference, and everyday judgment. The question is important because it tests how far perception can justify beliefs about reality versus how much interpretation, uncertainty, and error remain built in. The analysis synthesizes **11 cited sources** across philosophy (e.g., analytic debates, transcendental idealism) and cognitive/neuroscience evidence, using a comparative, citation-grounded approach that triangulates conceptual arguments with experimental findings.

- **Perception is structurally underdetermined:** sensory input alone does not uniquely specify the world, so the brain must infer using priors and constraints (inverse-problem framing) [10].  
- **Illusion and hallucination remain decisive pressure points:** mainstream philosophy still treats veridical and non-veridical experience as a core unresolved tension, weakening strong direct-access claims [4][2].  
- **Attention imposes practical access limits:** in inattentional-blindness experiments (`n=192`), nearly half of participants missed salient unexpected events, showing substantial real-time perceptual omission [6].  
- **Perception is actively constructed over time:** saccadic suppression and filling-in show periodic information loss and percept generation beyond local retinal input, separating stimulus from experience [7][8].

**Primary conclusion: current evidence supports constrained, corrigible contact with reality, not a total collapse of the perception–perceived gap; the gap can be reduced but not eliminated because perception is perspectival, model-based, and revisable** [3][4][5][6][10][11].

Notable gaps include weak evidence for self–other perception discrepancies in the provided corpus (largely descriptive/non-peer-reviewed) [1], limited integration of real-world ecological complexity, and no single framework that fully reconciles philosophical and empirical accounts.
