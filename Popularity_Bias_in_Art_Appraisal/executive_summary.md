# Executive Summary

**Sources analyzed:** 7 | **Grounding Score:** 32% | **Sub-Questions:** 0 (0 answered)

---

This report asks whether art appraisal is driven more by intrinsic qualities of artworks or by social signals such as artist fame, moral reputation, and attribution labels, and why that matters for cultural legitimacy and market pricing. The question is important because institutions, collectors, and audiences often treat prices and prestige as proxies for quality, which may systematically distort recognition and reward.

Methodologically, the report synthesizes evidence from **7 cited sources** ([6], [7], [8], [9], [10], [11], [13]) using a mixed approach: controlled experiments (including large behavioral and preregistered designs) plus historical/market case analysis to compare label effects against relatively unchanged underlying works.

- **Social-proof cues can dominate quality signals: in a 14,341-participant music-market experiment, popularity visibility increased inequality and unpredictability of success** [6].  
- **Artist metadata changes judgments directly: negative biographical information reduced liking and perceived quality, including for famous and unknown artists** [7].  
- Attribution and fame premiums are large: identical works labeled as by famous artists were rated more positively and valued more highly (including willingness to pay) [7].  
- High-profile market cases (e.g., *Salvator Mundi*, Beeple) and historical reversals (Impressionists, Lennon backlash, Trumbo blacklist) show valuation/recognition can swing sharply with status, controversy, or stigma rather than object-level change [8][9][10][11][13].

**Primary conclusion: art judgment is often social judgment first; fame and prestige can inflate perceived quality, while moral or political stigma can suppress it, even when the work itself is unchanged** [6][7].

Key limitations include small samples in some experimental components, reliance on prominent anecdotal cases for external validity, limited ability to measure “objective” quality shifts, and uncertainty about long-run versus short-run effects across contexts.
