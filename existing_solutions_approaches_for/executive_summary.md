# Executive Summary

**Sources analyzed:** 3 | **Grounding Score:** 8% | **Sub-Questions:** 0 (0 answered)

---

This report asks which “extra senses” can be added today in a practical, evidence-backed way, and why that matters for real-world mobility, orientation, and assistive independence. The question is important because many augmentation concepts are still speculative, while users need deployable options with demonstrated outcomes now. The synthesis draws on nine cited sources ([1], [3]–[9]) and uses a comparative evidence-and-availability approach: pairing peer-reviewed or regulatory outcomes with product-level implementation signals to identify solutions that are both effective and obtainable.

- **Directional augmentation is already viable in daily use:** in a 7-week feelSpace study, most users reported a new spatial sense and showed statistically significant improvement in orientation in unfamiliar settings (χ²(6)=20.54, p=0.002) [4].  
- **Continuous “north sense” products are commercially available today**, with implant-anchored directional feedback described as persistent rather than intermittent compass checking [9].  
- **Vibrotactile balance substitution has the strongest durability signal:** bilateral vestibulopathy users improved quickly (median BMS 4 to 8 at 2 weeks) and retained meaningful gains at 2 years (median +4) [5].  
- FDA De Novo classification of BrainPort (DEN130039) and reported commercial rollout indicate regulatory and market precedent for non-visual sensory channels [6][7]; WeWALK adds practical head-level obstacle detection but with weaker peer-reviewed efficacy depth [8].

**Primary conclusion: the most defensible two-sense strategy today is non-invasive sensory substitution combining continuous directional north cues with continuous obstacle/proprioceptive haptic feedback, because this pair best aligns current evidence strength with real-world availability** [4][5][8][9].

Key limitations are uneven evidence maturity across technologies, limited consumer-ready validation for olfactory/taste augmentation, and reliance on some product claims where independent outcome studies remain sparse [1][3][8][9].
