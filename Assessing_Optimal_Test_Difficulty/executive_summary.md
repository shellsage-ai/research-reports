This report asks: what is the “optimal” test difficulty, and should standards be made universally stricter (for item difficulty and statistical significance)? This matters because test rigor directly affects decision accuracy, fairness across populations, false-positive risk, and practical feasibility in educational and research settings. The analysis synthesizes **6 cited sources** using a comparative, evidence-weighting approach across psychometrics (CTT/equating, PISA proficiency design) and statistical-inference guidance (ASA/CONSORT and empirical threshold re-analyses) [1]–[6].

- **Item difficulty is population-relative, not absolute**: in CTT, difficulty is the proportion correct (`p`), and the same item can appear easier/harder across cohorts; ETS equating methods are used to adjust these group differences [2].  
- **Tests perform best with calibrated spread, not uniform hardness**: evidence indicates mid-range difficulty (around `p = 0.50`) supports discrimination, while all-easy/all-hard items provide less differentiation; PISA also targets level-relative performance patterns near the boundary rather than one fixed hardness point [2][3].  
- **Measurement quality depends on discrimination as well as difficulty**: items with weak or negative discrimination should be reviewed, with ~0.20 commonly cited as a practical target and <0.10 concerning [5].  
- **Stricter significance cutoffs reduce false positives but impose major costs**: repeated unadjusted testing can inflate error rates, while moving from 0.05 to 0.005 can sharply reduce “significant” results and require much larger samples [3][4][5][6].

**Primary conclusion: the best-supported strategy is predeclared, context-calibrated rigor (often starting at α = 0.05), not blanket “harder by default” thresholds** [1][2][3].

Notable gaps include limited domain-specific implementation guidance, potential context dependence of cited thresholds, and unresolved trade-off rules for choosing stricter standards under resource constraints.
