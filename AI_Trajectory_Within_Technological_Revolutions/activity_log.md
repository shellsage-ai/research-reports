- [06:13:30] **Created**: Job created for prompt: RESEARCH OBJECTIVE
Determine whether Artificial Intelligence represents a continuation of historical technological patterns, a faster-but-predictable acceleration, or a categorically different kind of revolution — using measurable historical evidence as the analytical foundation.

CENTRAL HYPOTHESIS TO TEST
Technological revolutions follow identifiable mathematical patterns (S-curves, exponential phases, punctuated equilibrium). If AI follows the same underlying dynamics as electricity, the internet, and the printing press, its trajectory and societal impact are broadly predictable. If it deviates from those patterns in kind — not just magnitude — then existing forecasting frameworks must be revised.

PRIMARY RESEARCH QUESTIONS

1. PATTERN IDENTIFICATION
   - What measurable growth curves characterize major technological transitions across history?
   - Do technologies universally progress through the same lifecycle phases (invention → infrastructure → mass adoption → plateau)? Find exceptions.
   - How does Wright's Law (learning curves) compare to Moore's Law (calendar-based doubling) as a predictive model for AI capability growth?

2. ACCELERATION TRIGGERS
   - What specific conditions (capital concentration, geopolitical pressure, scientific convergence, regulatory absence) reliably precede rapid acceleration?
   - Which of those conditions are currently present for AI, and which are absent?
   - Are there historical cases where all acceleration conditions were present but breakthrough was delayed or failed entirely? What caused the failure?

3. COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS — AI vs. PRIOR TRANSFORMATIVE TECHNOLOGIES
   - Printing press: information distribution. Steam engine: physical labor. Electricity: energy infrastructure. Internet: communication. Compare AI's adoption curve and societal disruption rate against each at equivalent stages.
   - At the same point in their development timelines, which prior technology does AI most closely resemble — and where does the comparison break down?
   - What is the median lag between a transformative technology's emergence and its measurable GDP/productivity impact? Where is AI in that lag curve today?

4. DISTINGUISHING FEATURES (WHERE AI MAY DEVIATE FROM PATTERN)
   - Prior transformative technologies amplified human output in a specific domain. AI's claimed scope is general cognitive augmentation. Has any prior technology claimed general scope and delivered it? Evaluate historical analogies.
   - Recursive self-improvement (AI systems contributing to AI development) has no historical precedent. How do classical S-curve and diffusion models account for or fail to account for this dynamic?
   - Does the speed of AI capability gain (months, not decades) invalidate historical institutional adaptation timelines? Find quantified evidence.

5. BOTTLENECKS AND CEILINGS
   - What halted or slowed previous technology S-curves? (Energy limits, material constraints, regulatory capture, economic saturation, war.)
   - What are the current measurable bottlenecks for AI: compute cost, energy infrastructure, data quality, regulatory, talent concentration, or capital access?
   - Which bottleneck is most likely to be binding within a 5-year horizon? Cite evidence from primary sources.

6. SOCIETAL AND ECONOMIC IMPLICATIONS BY TIMEFRAME
   - 5–10 years: What labor market shifts are already measurable in early-adopter sectors? Project forward using prior technology diffusion rates.
   - 10–25 years: Based on electricity/internet precedents, what second- and third-order effects emerge after infrastructure saturation?
   - Long-term (25+ years): Under what conditions does AI follow the electricity model (ubiquitous, invisible, productivity-enabling) vs. a more disruptive or unstable trajectory?

SCOPE
- Time range: Agricultural Revolution (~10,000 BCE) through 2025
- Technologies for direct comparison: printing press, steam engine, electricity, telephone, internal combustion engine, internet, smartphones
- Economic frameworks: GPT (General Purpose Technology) theory, Kondratiev waves, diffusion of innovations (Rogers), Perez technological surge cycles
- Quantitative sources required: patent filing rates, R&D spend, productivity indices (TFP), adoption curves, capital formation data, academic publication velocity

EVIDENCE STANDARDS
- Prioritize peer-reviewed economic history and empirical technology diffusion studies
- Flag all speculative claims as distinct from evidence-backed findings
- Require citation for every claimed historical date, growth rate, or adoption figure
- Surface and report conflicting evidence — do not suppress findings that undermine the central hypothesis

DELIVERABLE FORMAT
- Executive summary: verdict on the central hypothesis (continuation / faster acceleration / categorically different), with confidence level
- Section per PRIMARY RESEARCH QUESTION with findings, supporting evidence, and dissenting evidence
- Comparative timeline: AI vs. prior technologies at equivalent development stages
- Predictive model section: 3 scenarios (conservative/base/accelerated) with stated assumptions for each
- Mermaid diagram: technology adoption S-curves overlaid for all comparison technologies
- Uncertainty log: list the 5 questions where evidence was thinnest or most contested
