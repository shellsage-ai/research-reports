This report asks: how should a five-year (2026–2031) crypto portfolio be structured to balance survivability and upside across uncertain market regimes? The question matters because crypto returns are highly path-dependent, dominated by liquidity cycles, regulation, and network-level adoption rather than single-asset narratives.

Methodologically, the report synthesizes **12 cited sources** ([1]–[12]) using an evidence-weighted, scenario-based approach: market structure and valuation snapshots, drawdown history, adoption/value-capture indicators (transactions, TVL, REV), developer durability metrics, and regulatory/institutional flow data. It then translates these into portfolio construction implications via stress-tested Bear/Base/Bull framing.

Most important findings:
- **Crypto market concentration remains decisive (BTC ~57.4%, ETH ~10.2%), so large-cap flow regimes are likely to drive five-year outcomes more than smaller-alt narratives** [1].
- **Historical drawdowns for BTC/ETH/SOL/LINK (roughly -42% to -82% vs ATH) imply portfolios must be explicitly built to survive 70–90% shock conditions, not just optimize expected return** [1][2][3][4].
- **Adoption data supports ETH and SOL as differentiated execution-layer exposures (Ethereum scale/TVL leadership vs Solana growth/REV strength), suggesting a scale-vs-growth barbell rather than winner-take-all** [5][7].
- **Regulatory access and institutional flows are strongest in BTC/ETH (including spot ETP approvals and in-kind mechanisms), reinforcing core-asset liquidity and allocators’ preference hierarchy** [10][11][12].

**Primary conclusion: a core-heavy BTC/ETH allocation with measured SOL/LINK satellites is the most defensible five-year structure, and success depends on probabilistic sizing/rebalancing discipline rather than point forecasts** [1][2][3][4][10][11][12].

Key limitations include reliance on point-in-time snapshots, uncertain policy/macro shifts, potential source-timing mismatch across datasets, and limited treatment of tail risks (custody, smart-contract, governance, and cross-chain contagion).
